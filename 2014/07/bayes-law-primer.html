Bayes' Law Primer

Jul 29, 2014

I'm currently writing a blog post that uses Bayes' Law but don't want to muddy the post with a review in layman's terms. So I have something to link, here is a short description and a chance to flex my teaching muscles before the school year starts.

Bayes' Law

For those who aren't sure, Bayes' Law tells us that the probability event XXX occurs given we know that event YYY has occurred can easily be computed. It is written as Pr(X ∣ Y)\text{Pr}(X \, | \, Y)Pr(X∣Y) vertical bar is meant like the word "given", in other words, the event XXX is distinct from the event (X ∣ Y),(X \, | \, Y),(X∣Y), i.e. XXX given YYY. Bayes' law, states that

Pr(X ∣ Y)=Pr(X and Y both occur)Pr(Y).\text{Pr}(X \, | \, Y) = \frac{\text{Pr}(X \text{ and } Y \text{ both occur})}{\text{Pr}(Y)}.Pr(X∣Y)=Pr(Y)Pr(X and Y both occur)​.

This effectively is a re-scaling of the events by the total probability of the given event: Pr(Y)\text{Pr}(Y)Pr(Y).

For example, if XXX is the event that a 333 is rolled on a fair die and YYY is the event that the roll is odd. We know of course that Pr(Y)=12\text{Pr}(Y) = \frac{1}{2}Pr(Y)=21​ since half of the rolls are odd. The event X and Y both occurX \text{ and } Y \text{ both occur}X and Y both occur in this case is the same as XXX since the roll can only be 333 if the roll is already odd. Thus

Pr(X and Y both occur)=16\text{Pr}(X \text{ and } Y \text{ both occur}) = \frac{1}{6}Pr(X and Y both occur)=61​

and we can compute the conditional probability

Pr(X ∣ Y)=1/61/2=13.\text{Pr}(X \, | \, Y) = \frac{1 / 6}{1 / 2} = \frac{1}{3}.Pr(X∣Y)=1/21/6​=31​.

As we expect, one out of every three odd rolls is a 333.

Bayes' Law Extended Form

Instead of considering a single event Y,Y,Y, we can consider a range of nnn possible events Y1,Y2,…,YnY_1, Y_2, \ldots, Y_nY1​,Y2​,…,Yn​ occur. We require that one of these YYY-events must occur and that they cover all possible events that could occur. For example Y1Y_1Y1​ is the event that H2O is vapor, Y2Y_2Y2​ is the event that H2O is water and Y3Y_3Y3​ is the event that H2O is ice.

In such a case we know that since the YYY-events are distinct

Pr(X)=Pr(X and Y1 both occur)+Pr(X and Y2 both occur)+Pr(X and Y3 both occur).\begin{aligned} \text{Pr}(X) &= \text{Pr}(X \text{ and } Y_1 \text{ both occur}) \\ &+ \text{Pr}(X \text{ and } Y_2 \text{ both occur}) \\ &+ \text{Pr}(X \text{ and } Y_3 \text{ both occur}). \end{aligned}Pr(X)​=Pr(X and Y1​ both occur)+Pr(X and Y2​ both occur)+Pr(X and Y3​ both occur).​

Using Bayes' law, we can reinterpret as

Pr(X and Yj both occur)=Pr(X ∣ Yj)⋅Pr(Yj)\text{Pr}(X \text{ and } Y_j \text{ both occur}) = \text{Pr}(X \, | \, Y_j) \cdot \text{Pr}(Y_j)Pr(X and Yj​ both occur)=Pr(X∣Yj​)⋅Pr(Yj​)

and the above becomes

Pr(X)=Pr(X ∣ Y1)⋅Pr(Y1)+Pr(X ∣ Y2)⋅Pr(Y2)+Pr(X ∣ Y3)⋅Pr(Y3).\begin{aligned} \text{Pr}(X) &= \text{Pr}(X \, | \, Y_1) \cdot \text{Pr}(Y_1) + \text{Pr}(X \, | \, Y_2) \cdot \text{Pr}(Y_2) \\ &+ \text{Pr}(X \, | \, Y_3) \cdot \text{Pr}(Y_3). \end{aligned}Pr(X)​=Pr(X∣Y1​)⋅Pr(Y1​)+Pr(X∣Y2​)⋅Pr(Y2​)+Pr(X∣Y3​)⋅Pr(Y3​).​

The same is true if we replace 333 with an arbitrary number of events nnn.
